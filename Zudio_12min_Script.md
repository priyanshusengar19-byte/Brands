# 12-Minute Presentation Script
### India's Organized Apparel Frontier: The T2/T3 Tipping Point
**Speaker: Priyanshu Sengar | Bernstein — Summer 2026**

## [SLIDE 1 — COVER]
*~30 seconds*

Good morning, everyone.

I want to start with a simple question. When you think of fashion retail in India — the stores, the brands, the growth — where do you picture it happening?

Most people picture Mumbai. Delhi. Bangalore. The big cities with the malls and the high streets.

That picture is wrong. And over the next twelve minutes, I want to show you exactly where the real opportunity is — and why one company is sitting right at the centre of it.

---

## [SLIDE 3 — WHAT'S DRIVING THIS ANALYSIS]
*~1 min | Running total: ~1:30*

Before I get into numbers, let me tell you why this analysis exists at all.

Three things caught our attention.

**First** — when you compare how much of India's clothing spending goes through proper organised stores versus how much goes through local shops and street markets, India is far behind countries with similar income levels. Countries at a similar stage of economic development typically have around [BLANK]% of their clothing market going through organised stores. India is at roughly 20%. That gap doesn't stay forever. It closes — and when it does, it creates enormous value for the players who are already there.

**Second** — within organised apparel, one segment has been taking market share from everyone else. Everyday affordable clothing — what the industry calls value fashion — gained six percentage points of organised market share in just five years. No other category came close.

**Third** — the pace of new store openings has nearly doubled. India added 808 net new organised stores in FY24. In FY25, that number jumped to 1,571. In a single year, the industry opened twice as many stores.

Three signals, all pointing the same direction. We decided to follow them.

---

## [SLIDE 4 — INDIA'S ORGANISED APPAREL STORY HAS JUST BEGUN]
*~1 min | Running total: ~2:30*

Here's the full picture of how big this market is.

India's total clothing and footwear market — everything from a designer label to your neighbourhood tailor — was worth roughly ₹10.38 lakh crore in FY26. For those doing the math in dollars, that is approximately **124 billion dollars**. By next year, FY27, it is projected to reach ₹12.17 lakh crore — close to **145 billion dollars**.

This is one of the largest clothing markets on earth. And it is still growing.

Now here's what makes it interesting from an investment standpoint. Of all that money, only 45% flows through organised stores today. Four years ago it was 38%. It is rising every single year, and the companies picking up that shift are the organised players.

Of every 100 net new organised stores that opened last year, 43 were value fashion stores. Value fashion is not just growing — it is leading the entire organised retail expansion in this country.

---

## [SLIDE 5 — THE WALLET DOESN'T FOLLOW THE POPULATION]
*~45 sec | Running total: ~3:15*

Before I tell you which companies are winning, I need to show you something that completely changes how you should think about this market.

You would naturally assume that clothing spending follows population density — more people, more spending. India has over a billion people living outside its major cities. But what percentage of India's clothing spending do you think happens there?

It's 35%. The smaller cities and towns — what the industry calls Tier 3, Tier 4, and beyond — hold **35% of all clothing spending in India**.

Meanwhile, the big metros and top cities together hold only about 25%.

The money is not where most brands have built their stores. That is the single most important insight in this entire presentation.

---

## [SLIDES 6–7 — VALUE FASHION: WHERE THE TAILWIND IS STRONGEST]
*~1:15 min | Running total: ~4:30*

So now the question is: which type of clothing business is best placed to go after that money?

The answer, very clearly, is value fashion.

Here's why. Value fashion holds 28% of the total organised apparel market. It grows at 13.7% every year — faster than mid-market, faster than premium, faster than everything else. And despite being the biggest and fastest-growing segment, there are only **5 major listed players** competing in it nationally.

Let me put that in context. The mid-price clothing segment — your ₹1,000 to ₹5,000 range — has 42 different brands fighting for the same customer. Forty-two. In value fashion, it's 15.

And here's the kicker: the organised, listed players in value fashion already earn more revenue per store than the smaller, unorganised ones. So not only is value fashion the least crowded segment — it's also where the best players earn the most.

Less competition. Faster growth. Higher earnings per store. That is the tailwind.

---

## [SLIDE 8 — THE WALLET AND THE STORES ARE IN DIFFERENT PLACES]
*~45 sec | Running total: ~5:15*

Now let's put the opportunity in front of you in the starkest possible way.

We mapped every single organised apparel store in India to the pincode where it sits, and we compared that to where India's actual clothing spending happens.

Metro cities have **32% of all organised stores**. But only **15%** of clothing spending happens there. They are over-served.

Tier 3 cities — the mid-sized towns that most brands still treat as secondary markets — hold **24% of India's clothing spending**. But only **16% of organised stores** are there. They are massively under-served.

The money is in Tier 3. The stores are in Metro. That mismatch — that is the opportunity.

---

## [SLIDE 10 — THE THESIS: ONE SENTENCE, THEN THE EVIDENCE]
*~30 sec | Running total: ~5:45*

So here is our thesis in one sentence:

Tier 2 and Tier 3 cities hold **42% of India's total clothing spending** — roughly ₹3.5 lakh crore, or about **42 billion dollars** — but they have half the store density per rupee of spending compared to Metro cities.

That gap is the investment case. And value fashion — with its lower price point, lower store costs, and higher volume model — is the only format that can close it profitably.

---

## [SLIDE 11 — HOW WE BUILT THE GROUND-UP FRAMEWORK]
*~45 sec | Running total: ~6:30*

Now, I want to take 45 seconds to tell you how we arrived at our numbers. Because the method matters.

Most market reports start with a national estimate and work their way down. We did the complete opposite.

We started with all **19,742 pincodes** in India. For each one, we calculated the clothing spending based on local income data — not national averages.

Then we scraped and mapped more than **31,000 organised stores** across every brand, every tier, every city, pincode by pincode.

Then we matched the two — here's how much spending exists in this pincode, here's how much organised retail is capturing, here's the gap.

This is not a top-down guess. This is a pincode-level answer to a very specific question: where is the money, and where are the stores missing?

---

## [SLIDES 12, 14, 15 — THE FRAMEWORK OUTPUT AND WHAT IT SHOWS]
*~1 min | Running total: ~7:30*

When we ran the numbers, a few things stood out.

For the total organised apparel market, our model shows India can support somewhere between **62,000 and 63,000 viable organised stores** — roughly double the 31,000 that exist today. The market is only halfway built.

Now, is all of that available to every brand? No. We filtered for value fashion specifically — and further filtered to look at Zudio's economics.

But before we get there, one more thing I want you to see.

Despite all this growth, roughly **80% of India's clothing spending still goes to unorganised shops** — local tailors, market stalls, wholesale bazaars. Even in big cities, close to 35% is still unorganised. In smaller towns and rural areas, it's over 80%.

The organised sector is not running out of room. It is running out of excuses for not going there.

---

## [SLIDES 16–19 — THE PINCODE EVIDENCE]
*~1:30 min | Running total: ~9:00*

Let me now show you what this looks like at the street level.

Right now, **80% of India's pincodes** — 4 out of every 5 — do not have a single organised clothing store. Not one.

And here is the number that honestly surprised us: even in pincodes where people spend more than ₹300 crore a year on clothing — that is over 3.5 million dollars — **20% of those pincodes still have zero organised stores**. These are not poor areas. They are well-off areas that have simply been ignored.

Of the pincodes that do have at least one organised store, 85% have fewer than 4. The market is not saturated. It's barely started.

Now you might ask — is there a ceiling? At some point does the market fill up?

Yes, there is a ceiling. We modelled it. And even at maximum build-out, most pincodes will have **5 or fewer organised stores**. The market never becomes crowded.

Here is another way to see this. In Metro cities, the organised market has already captured about **80% of its potential**. Tier 1 cities are at **83%** — also nearly full. But Tier 2 cities are only **44%** through their potential. And Tier 3 cities? **21%.** That means **79% of Tier 3's entire opportunity is still open.**

This is not the end of a growth story. It is the middle.

---

## [SLIDE 20 — ARE THEY FOLLOWING THE TREND?]
*~45 sec | Running total: ~9:45*

So which brands are actually set up to go after this?

We looked at over 100 organised apparel brands and categorised them by where they are building their stores. Only **11 brands** are structurally positioned to benefit from the Tier 2/Tier 3 shift. Of those 11, **6 are value fashion brands**.

Zudio is one of them. **71% of Zudio's stores are already in Tier 2 cities and below.** That is not an accident — that is strategy.

And what the data shows is that Zudio earns significantly more per store than its value fashion peers. So it's in the right places, and it's more productive when it gets there.

---

## [SLIDES 22–23 — IS ZUDIO EXPANDING TOO FAST OR NOT FAST ENOUGH?]
*~1:15 min | Running total: ~11:00*

This brings us to the central question of this presentation: is Zudio moving too fast, or not fast enough?

Zudio currently has about **960 stores**. In FY26, it generated **₹12,215 crore in revenue — approximately 1.45 billion dollars**.

Our analysis shows that India can support roughly **2,962 Zudio stores** — excluding rural areas, where the supply chain economics don't yet make sense for fewer than 5 stores per market. That is **three times more than today**.

If Zudio reaches that number while maintaining current store productivity, the revenue ceiling is **₹47,316 crore — approximately 5.6 billion dollars**. The gap between where it is now and that ceiling is ₹35,101 crore — roughly **4.2 billion dollars of revenue that has not been built yet.**

To get there in 10 years, Zudio needs to add about 233 stores per year. Its current pace is around 350. **The pace is not the problem. The question is whether those new stores are going to the right places.**

Every year a Tier 3 city doesn't get a Zudio store is a year the local unorganised market keeps that customer. And in a business where people are still forming their habits — still deciding which brand they trust — losing that year has a cost that doesn't go away.

---

## [SLIDE 24 — WHAT IS THE TAKEAWAY?]
*~1 min | Running total: ~12:00*

Let me leave you with four things.

**One — the expansion story has not peaked.** The market can absorb three times more Zudio stores than exist today. No macroeconomic growth assumed. No income tailwind. Just today's numbers applied to the white space that already exists.

**Two — being first in a Tier 3 city compounds over time.** Value fashion nationally has 5 serious players. Every new city Zudio enters makes it harder for the next brand to come in and take that customer. First movers build loyalty that is very expensive to undo.

**Three — the revenue potential is ₹47,316 crore, roughly 5.6 billion dollars, at today's income levels.** That number gets bigger every year as India gets richer — and we haven't assumed any of that in our model.

**Four — every slow year is a structural loss, not just a missed sales number.** In a market where brand loyalty is still forming, the window to be first is finite. Waiting has a permanent cost.

India's organised clothing story has barely started. The Tier 2 and Tier 3 wave is the next chapter. And based on everything we found — the data, the store mapping, the spending analysis — Zudio is the brand best placed to write it.

Thank you.
