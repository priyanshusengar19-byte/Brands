### India's Organized Apparel Frontier: The T2/T3 Tipping Point

## [SLIDE 1 — COVER]
*~20 seconds*

Good morning everyone.

I want to start with a question that looks simple but isn't.

India's clothing market is one of the largest in the world. Brands are opening stores faster than ever. So why does the data show that 80% of India's postal codes — 4 out of 5 — don't have a single organised clothing store?

That is the question this presentation tries to answer. And the answer changes how you think about where the real opportunity is.

---

## [SLIDE 2 — WHAT'S DRIVING THIS ANALYSIS]
*~1 min 10 sec | Running total: ~1:30*

Three things made us build this analysis.

The first is a structural gap. India's organised apparel market has reached about 48% penetration — meaning roughly half of all clothing spending flows through proper chain stores. Countries at a similar stage of development — China, Japan, Indonesia — are well above that. India is behind, which means it has further to travel. And that journey creates value for whoever is already positioned in the right places.

The second thing is a market share shift that most people missed. Everyday affordable clothing — what the industry calls value fashion — has gained 6 percentage points of organised market share in the last 5 years. No other segment came close.

The third thing is the pace of change. In FY24, India added 808 net new organised stores. In FY25, that jumped to 1,571. And in FY26, value fashion alone was responsible for 43% of every new organised store that opened in the country.

Three signals. All pointing the same way. We decided to follow them.

---

## [SLIDE 3 — INDIA'S ORGANISED APPAREL STORY HAS JUST BEGUN]
*~1 min | Running total: ~2:30*

Here is the scale of what we are talking about.

India's total clothing and footwear market was 108 billion dollars in FY26. By next year it is projected to reach 128 billion dollars. It is one of the largest clothing markets on earth and it is still growing fast.

What makes it interesting is not just the size — it is who is doing the growing. Of that total market, only 45% flows through organised stores today, up from 38% four years ago. Organised retail is taking share from local markets every single year.

And look at the right chart. India's per capita spending on clothing is growing at 16% a year — the fastest among all the comparable markets we looked at. China and Japan are mature markets with much lower growth. India and Indonesia are where the energy is.

The organised clothing story in India has barely started.

---

## [SLIDE 4 — THE WALLET DOESN'T FOLLOW THE POPULATION]
*~45 sec | Running total: ~3:15*

Now here is the thing that most retail brands get wrong about India.

Look at this chart. It shows what share of India's total clothing spending comes from each tier of cities.

Metro cities hold 15%. Tier 1 cities hold 10%. But Tier 2 cities hold 17%, Tier 3 cities hold 24%, and Tier 4 and smaller towns hold 35%. That is a 96 billion dollar spending pool sitting in smaller cities and towns — larger than any other single tier.

T2 and T3 together hold 41% of India's entire clothing wallet — roughly 40 billion dollars. Bigger than Metro and T1 combined.

The money is not in the big cities. That is the entire story.

---

## [SLIDE 5 — VALUE FASHION: WHERE THE TAILWIND IS STRONGEST (Part 1)]
*~1 min | Running total: ~4:15*

So given all this, which type of clothing business is best placed to go after that opportunity?

The answer is value fashion. And here is why.

Value fashion holds 28% of the total organised apparel market — the largest single segment. It grows at 13.7% a year. And there are only 5 major national players competing in it.

Now look at the right chart — this is the one that really matters. It shows what percentage of each segment's revenue flows through organised brand-owned stores.

Value fashion went from 17.6% to 18.9% in just one year. It is going up.

Mid-market went from 14.7% down to 13.6%. It is going down.

Premium went from 18.1% down to 16%. Also going down.

Value fashion is the only segment where organised retailers are actively growing their share — not just of a growing market, but of their own segment. That is what makes it structurally different from everything else.

---

## [SLIDE 6 — VALUE FASHION: WHERE THE TAILWIND IS STRONGEST (Part 2)]
*~45 sec | Running total: ~5:00*

And here is the competitive picture.

In the mid-market — the ₹1,000 to ₹5,000 clothing range — 42 brands are fighting for the same customer. In value fashion, it is 15 brands. Less than half.

And of those 15 brands in value, the organised listed players — the ones with real infrastructure, proper stores, and national presence — earn significantly more revenue per store than their smaller unlisted rivals. So the market is not just less crowded. The best players are more productive within it.

Less competition, faster growth, higher earnings per store. That is why value fashion is where the tailwind is strongest.

---

## [SLIDE 7 — THE WALLET AND THE STORES ARE IN DIFFERENT PLACES]
*~45 sec | Running total: ~5:45*

Now let me show you the gap in one chart.

We mapped every organised clothing store in India and compared it against where the actual spending happens.

Metro cities have 32% of all organised stores. But only 15% of India's clothing spending happens there. They are over-invested.

T2 and T3 cities together have 37% of stores. But they hold 41% of spending. They are under-served.

The money moved to the smaller cities. The stores did not follow.

That gap — between where spending is concentrated and where stores exist — is the investment opportunity this entire analysis is built around.

---

## [SLIDE 8 — THE THESIS: ONE SENTENCE, THEN THE EVIDENCE]
*~25 sec | Running total: ~6:10*

So here is the thesis.

T2 and T3 cities hold 41% of India's clothing spending — roughly 40 billion dollars. But they get half the store density per rupee of opportunity compared to Metro cities. And value fashion, with its lower price point and lower cost structure, is the only format that can profitably close that gap at scale.

That is the one sentence. Everything else in this presentation is the evidence.

---

## [SLIDE 9 — HOW WE BUILT THE GROUND-UP FRAMEWORK]
*~40 sec | Running total: ~6:50*

Before I show you the evidence, let me tell you how we built it — because the method matters.

Most market analyses start from a national number and divide it. We did the opposite.

We started with all 19,742 postal codes in India. For each one, we calculated the clothing spending based on local income data — not national averages. Then we scraped and mapped over 31,000 organised stores, pincode by pincode, brand by brand.

Then we matched the two — here is the spending in this pincode, here is how much organised retail is capturing, here is the rupee gap.

This is not a market report. It is a pincode-level answer to a specific question: where is the money, and where are the stores missing?

---

## [SLIDE 10 — HOW WE CALCULATED TOTAL POTENTIAL STORES]
*~40 sec | Running total: ~7:30*

From that data, we built a two-track model.

Track one calculates how many organised stores the total Indian market can viably support — based on spending per pincode, how much of that spending organised stores typically capture at maturity, and average revenue per store. The output is filled in on your slides — check it against the number shown.

Track two narrows it down to Zudio specifically — using their current market position within value fashion and their revenue per store advantage.

One important note in the assumptions box — Tier 4 and rural areas are excluded from both outputs. A brand needs a minimum cluster of stores in a geography to justify the supply chain investment. Below 5 stores per market, the economics simply don't work, regardless of how much spending exists there. That is why the T4+ bar appears in gray on the store potential charts.

---

## [SLIDE 11 — INDIA'S TRUE APPAREL AND FASHION WALLET]
*~30 sec | Running total: ~8:00*

This scatter chart shows something important about T3 specifically.

The vertical line is the viability threshold — roughly ₹6,500 of clothing spending per person per year is what it takes for an organised store to be financially viable.

T3 cities sit right on that threshold. They have enough spending per person to support organised retail. But almost no stores relative to that spending. They are the most underleveraged point on this entire chart.

T4+ falls below the threshold — which is exactly why it appears in gray in our store potential charts. The spending isn't deep enough yet.

---

## [SLIDE 12 — THE MARKET IS STILL DOMINATED BY UNORGANISED TRADE]
*~40 sec | Running total: ~8:40*

Despite all the growth we have talked about, this chart shows how much of the market is still to be captured.

In Metro cities, about 48% of clothing spending flows through organised stores. That is the benchmark — the level every other tier is eventually moving toward.

In Tier 4 cities, only 19% is organised. 81% still goes to local tailors, street markets, and small shops.

And look at the right chart. Even in postal codes where people spend more than 300 crore rupees a year on clothing — well-off areas — 59% of those postal codes still have zero organised stores.

This is not a market at the end of its growth cycle. It is barely through the beginning.

---

## [SLIDE 13 — PINCODES WISE PENETRATION: HOW OPEN IS THE COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE]
*~40 sec | Running total: ~9:20*

Here is what the competitive landscape looks like at ground level.

Of all the postal codes that have at least one organised store, 63% have fewer than 4. The market is not just underpenetrated — it is effectively untouched.

1,325 postal codes have exactly one organised store. In those places, the first serious brand to arrive has no meaningful competition. And T2 and T3 combined hold 40% of those wide-open, lightly penetrated postal codes — the most open competitive landscape of any tier.

---

## [SLIDE 14 — HOW MANY STORES CAN BE REACHED]
*~35 sec | Running total: ~9:55*

So how big is the total opportunity?

Today India has about 31,800 organised stores. Our analysis shows the market can viably support around 51,000 — roughly 1.6 times the current base.

But here is the critical point: T2 and T3 together account for 79% of all the viable new additions. T3 alone accounts for 55%. The growth is not in the big cities. It has already happened there. The next phase of organised retail in India is a T2/T3 story.

---

## [SLIDE 15 — ARE THE STORES CORRECTLY PLACED?]
*~35 sec | Running total: ~10:30*

And are today's stores positioned for that story?

Metro cities are 80% through their total potential. Tier 1 cities are 83% through. Both nearly full.

Tier 2 cities are only 59% penetrated — 41% of the potential is still available. Tier 3 cities are at just 32% — which means 68% of T3's entire capacity for organised stores has not been built yet.

The industry has barely started on T3. That is where the runway is.

---

## [SLIDE 16 — HOW MUCH CAN WE ADD BEFORE SATURATION?]
*~30 sec | Running total: ~11:00*

The natural question is: does the market ever get full?

Right now, 80% of India's postal codes — 15,794 out of 19,742 — have zero organised retail. At full potential, 78% would have stores, up from 20% today. But even at that point, 64% of postal codes would still have 5 stores or fewer.

The market absorbs far more than currently exists, and it never becomes crowded. The only permanent floor is 22% of postal codes where household spending is simply too thin to support any organised store at current income levels.

---

## [SLIDE 17 — ARE THEY FOLLOWING THE TREND OR NOT?]
*~40 sec | Running total: ~11:40*

So which brands are actually set up to benefit?

We looked at over 100 organised apparel brands and classified them by expansion strategy. Only 11 are structurally positioned for the T2/T3 shift. Of those 11, 6 are in value fashion.

Now look at the brand table. Zudio earns $1.34 million per store — more than double V-Mart at $0.56 million, more than double Vishal at $0.72 million. The revenue per store advantage is significant.

But here is the honest read of this table: Zudio currently has 60% of its stores in Metro and T1 cities, and only 40% in T2, T3, and below. Its value fashion peers — V-Mart, Vishal, V2, Style Bazaar — are 69 to 78% T2+T3. Zudio is the most productive player in the segment. But it is not yet the most geographically aligned with the opportunity.

---

## [SLIDE 18 — IS ZUDIO EXPANDING TOO FAST OR NOT FAST ENOUGH? (Store Additions)]
*~40 sec | Running total: ~12:20*

That is exactly what the next two slides address.

Zudio has 958 stores today. Our bottom-up analysis shows it can grow to 1,860 — a 2x expansion at current store economics with no change in revenue per store assumed.

Of those 902 additional stores, 56% should go to Tier 3. That is where the white space is. Most of the new growth — 56% of it by new cities entirely — comes from geographies Zudio hasn't touched yet.

The T4+ bar is shown in gray and excluded from the 1,860 target for the same reason discussed earlier — supply chain economics below 5 stores per geography simply don't work.

---

## [SLIDE 19 — THE QUESTION: IS ZUDIO EXPANDING TOO FAST OR NOT FAST ENOUGH? (Revenue)]
*~55 sec | Running total: ~13:15 — trim from slide 18 if over time*

And here is what that means in revenue.

Zudio generated 1.29 billion dollars in FY26. The runway — the additional revenue available if it reaches its full store potential — is another 2.27 billion. The total ceiling at today's income levels is 3.55 billion dollars.

The chart on the right shows where that runway sits. T3 is where the largest gap is: only 19% penetrated, with 506 stores of runway remaining and 814 million dollars of revenue still to be captured from T3 alone.

And on the pace question — to capture that in 10 years requires opening 90 net stores a year. Zudio is currently running at well above that pace. The question is not whether they can open stores fast enough. The question is whether those stores are going to T3.

One more data point: Trent's own management guided for 5,000 Zudio stores over time at 200 additions per year at their most recent AGM. Our 1,860 store estimate is conservative by that measure.

---

## [SLIDE 20 — WHAT IS THE TAKEAWAY?]
*~50 sec | Running total: ~14:05 — cut 1 minute from earlier slides if needed*

Four things to leave with.

One — the expansion story has not peaked. The market absorbs 2 times more Zudio stores than exist today at current economics. No revenue growth assumed. No income tailwind. Just white space that already exists.

Two — being first in a T3 city compounds. Value fashion has 5 national players. Every T3 city Zudio enters raises the barrier for the next brand. In a market where loyalty is still forming, early presence is very hard to undo.

Three — the revenue potential is 3.55 billion dollars at today's income levels. That number only goes up as India gets richer.

Four — every slow year is a permanent position loss. Each year a T3 city doesn't get a Zudio store is a year the local unorganised market keeps that customer. In a brand-driven market, waiting has a cost that doesn't reverse.

India's organised apparel story has just begun. The T2/T3 wave is the next chapter. The data says Zudio is the best-positioned player to write it — but it has to actually go there.

Thank you.
