Framework & Rationale

◈ ASP Tier Definitions

  • Value (<₹400): Price-first proposition. Unbranded-adjacent. Targets Tier 2–4 cities, first-time branded buyers. Fast-fashion basics. Operators: V-Mart, Vishal, Style Bazaar.
  • Economy (₹400–₹900): Branded but budget. Accessible fashion with recognizable labels. Core of India's volume market. Zudio, Max, Reliance Trends fit here. ASP confirmed by Redseer's "sub-₹800 fastest-growing cluster."
  • Mid-Market (₹900–₹2,500): Quality-conscious, brand-loyal urban consumer. Includes Pantaloons, Westside, Levi's, H&M, and most "Friday-dressing" formal brands like Allen Solly & Peter England.
  • Premium (₹2,500–₹7,000): Aspirational, logo-driven, quality-fabric focus. Louis Philippe, Raymond, Van Heusen (formal), Tommy Hilfiger, Zara, Manyavar. International brand cachet or artisanal heritage.
  • Luxury (>₹7,000): Status-signalling, exclusive distribution, heritage positioning. Lacoste, Calvin Klein, Ritu Kumar. True luxury (Sabyasachi, Gucci) not in this list.

◈ Store Type Definitions

  • Western: Contemporary casual/trend-driven apparel — jeans, tees, dresses. D2C or mall-first. No strong ethnic or sport emphasis.
  • Formal: Primarily office/business/occasion wear — suits, formal shirts, trousers. Typically men's-first brands.
  • Sports: Performance or athleisure-first positioning — running, gym, training gear. EBOs and multi-sport retail.
  • Footwear: Footwear as core business; apparel is secondary or absent.
  • Department: Multi-brand or wide-category store. Carries apparel + accessories + lifestyle across genders and ages. Often houses multiple labels.
  • Hypermarket: Value-oriented mass-format retail. Large floor area, groceries/FMCG co-exist with apparel. Low-ticket impulse fashion.
  • Ethnic: Traditional Indian wear as primary category — kurtas, sarees, lehengas, sherwanis, salwar suits.

⚑ Major Calls — Where Two Categories Compete

Pantaloons → Mid-Market / Department
Pantaloons sells brands spanning Economy to Premium (LP, VH) but its own private labels and floor ASP sit firmly in the ₹900–₹2,000 range. Called Mid-Market. As a Department store it hosts multiple categories and brands — categorised as Department Store type, not Western or Formal, since it covers both.
H&M → Mid-Market (not Economy)
H&M entered India targeting the middle class with globally affordable prices. In India, its average ticket is ₹1,200–₹2,000, above Zudio/Max. Redseer and industry analysts position it in the mid-market internationally; in India its relative pricing lands it at the low end of Mid-Market. Economy would understate its brand premium perception.
Decathlon → Economy / Sports
Decathlon is a hypermarket-format sports specialist. Its prices span ₹300–₹3,000 but median performance apparel sits around ₹600–₹800. Called Economy ASP. Store type is Sports — it sells only sports-adjacent products, even if the format resembles a hypermarket physically.
Lacoste → Luxury (not Premium)
A polo at Lacoste India starts at ₹5,000 and core products hit ₹8,000–₹25,000. This comfortably crosses the ₹7,000 threshold. Globally positioned as "premium-luxury" but in India context, given its pricing relative to peers, Luxury is the right call. MBA Skool's own analysis calls Lacoste "premium luxury" with prestige pricing.
Fabindia → Mid-Market / Ethnic
Fabindia's price range (₹900–₹5,000) straddles Mid-Market and Premium. However, its median basket and everyday volume pricing is Mid-Market. It could be argued as Premium for its natural-fabric, artisanal positioning — but given mass footprint (357+ stores), Mid-Market is the better call. Ethnic is its clear store type.
Manyavar → Premium / Ethnic
Manyavar's wedding sherwanis and kurta sets average ₹3,000–₹12,000, with festive collections higher. Positioned as Premium. Mohey (its women's arm) follows the same logic. Store type is Ethnic — exclusively Indian occasion wear.
Shoppers Stop / Lifestyle → Department (not Western/Formal)
Both are multi-brand department stores carrying everything from formal to ethnic to sports brands. ASP depends entirely on which sub-brand/floor; the store itself is Mid-Market leaning. Type = Department.
Reliance Smart Bazaar → Value / Hypermarket
Smart Bazaar is a grocery-led hypermarket; apparel is a secondary, impulse category at very low price points. Separated from Reliance Trends (Economy / Western) on both dimensions.
Adidas / Nike / Puma → Premium / Sports
While these brands have entry-level products in the ₹1,500–₹2,000 range, their ASPs and brand positioning in India cluster around ₹3,000–₹6,000 for apparel. Premium is the right ASP. Contrast with Campus (Economy Sports) and Decathlon (Economy Sports).
Rare Rabbit → Premium / Western
Rare Rabbit sits at ₹2,500–₹5,000 per piece with a strong premium casual positioning and DTC-first strategy. Premium is the right call — a step above Levis/Flying Machine but below Zara's global premium image in India.
