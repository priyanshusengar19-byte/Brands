## [SLIDE: Value Fashion Is Driving India's Organized Retail Boom]
*~1 min*

Three things happened in Indian retail that made us build this analysis from scratch.

First — India's organized apparel penetration is at 48%. Countries at a similar stage — China, Japan, Indonesia — are all well above that. India is structurally behind, which means it has further to travel. And that gap creates value for whoever is already positioned correctly.

Second — within organized retail, one segment has been quietly taking everyone else's share. Everyday affordable clothing — value fashion — has gained 6 percentage points of organized market share in just 5 years. No other segment comes close.

Third — look at the chart. Net new organized store additions went from 808 in FY24 to 1,571 in FY25. Nearly double in a single year. And in FY26, 43 out of every 100 new organized stores that opened were value fashion stores.

These three signals all pointed the same direction. We decided to follow them.

---

## [SLIDE: Organized Retail Market Grew 2x Of Unorganized Retail Over Last 5 Years]
*~45 seconds*

Here's the scale. India's total clothing and footwear market was $108 billion in FY26. Next year it's heading to $128 billion.

But look at the right chart — this is the more important number for this room. India's per capita spending on clothing is growing at 16% a year. China is actually contracting at -2% in 2025. Japan and China are mature markets. India and Indonesia are where the growth is happening.

The organized share of this market has gone from 38% in FY23 to 48% projected by FY27. It is rising every single year.

## [SLIDE: T2+T3 Holds Around $40Bn Of Total Wallet]
*~30 seconds*

Now here is the thing most people get wrong about India.

The money is not in the big cities. T2 and T3 cities together hold 41% of India's clothing wallet — roughly $40 billion. T4+ alone holds $96 billion. That's bigger than any other single tier.

Metro cities hold 15%. Yet most stores are there.

Value fashion is the only segment where organized retailers are actively winning ground — not just in a growing market, but within their own segment.

## [SLIDE: 19,000+ Pincodes, 32,000+ Stores & Every Match Calculated]
*~1 min 30 seconds*

Most market reports start from a national number — take India's total apparel spend, assume an organized share percentage, divide by an average store size, and you get a store count. That is not what we did.

We started from zero. From the ground up.

Every postal code in India — 19,000 of them. For each one, we calculated how much clothing spending actually happens there, based on local income data. Not national averages. Not benchmark estimates. Actual local data, adjusted for the cost of living in that area.

Then we did something no market report had done — we scraped and mapped every single organized clothing store in India. Every brand. Every tier. Every pincode. 32,000+ stores, structured and matched to their location.

Then we asked — for this postal code, here is how much spending exists. Here is how much organized retail is currently capturing. What is the gap?

That gap, pincode by pincode, is what you are about to see.

There are four investment questions this framework was built to answer. How many postal codes are viable for organized stores but currently empty? Which spending brackets are over-penetrated and which are untouched? Are the big brands already filling the T2/T3 gap or is it genuinely open? And how many stores can India actually support?

We now have answers to all four.

---

## [SLIDE: The Two Outputs — Market Potential And Zudio's Ceiling]
*~1 min 30 seconds*

The model runs in two tracks.

Track one is market-level. We take the spending per postal code. We apply a saturation rate — how much of that spending can realistically flow through organized brand stores once the market matures, after removing what goes through ecommerce. We subtract what organized retail is already capturing. That gives us the rupee gap per postal code. Divide by the average revenue a store earns in that tier — adjusting for the fact that a Metro store earns more than a T3 store — and you get how many more stores that postal code can viably support.

One important assumption in the model — T4+ and rural areas are excluded. Not because the spending isn't there. It is. But a brand needs a minimum cluster of stores in a geography to justify the supply chain infrastructure. Below 5 stores per market, the logistics cost doesn't work regardless of how much wallet exists. That's why the T4+ bar appears in gray on every store potential chart.

Track two narrows to Zudio specifically. We take the value fashion share of the organized market in each pincode — roughly 28% — apply Zudio's current share within value fashion, and then adjust upward for the fact that Zudio earns significantly more per store than its peers.

Output A — total market. Output B — Zudio specifically. Both outputs are on this slide. I'll let the numbers speak in the next section.

## [SLIDE: Metro is More Than 90% Organized And T3 Below 40%]
*~45 seconds*

Let me show you what India's organized retail landscape actually looks like today.

Metro cities — over 90% organized. That is the mature state. That is what T2, T3, and every other tier will eventually look like as incomes rise and brands expand.

T3 cities — only 37% organized. 63% of spending still goes to local shops, street markets, and tailors.

And look at the right chart — organized retail stores physically reach only 31% of T3's population today. Less than 1 in 3 people in a T3 city have an organized clothing store anywhere near them.

This is not a market that has been tried and found difficult. It is a market that has barely been tried.

## [SLIDE: 1300+ Pincodes Have Just One Organized Store]
*~45 seconds*

Now zoom in further — to the postal code level.

1,325 postal codes in India have exactly one organized clothing store. One. The first brand that arrived, faces zero meaningful competition.

Another 1,165 have between 2 and 4 stores. So combined — 63% of all postal codes that have any organized presence at all have 4 stores or fewer.

And the right chart shows where this opportunity is thickest. T2 and T3 together account for 40% of all the lightly-penetrated postal codes — the ones with just 1 or 2 stores where first-mover advantage is still completely intact.

T4+ is excluded from this analysis for the supply chain reason I mentioned — the economics below 5 stores per geography simply don't work.

## [SLIDE: India Can Support 51,000 Organized Stores!]
*~45 seconds*

So how big is the total market opportunity?

India has about 31,800 organized clothing stores today. Our model says it can support just over 51,000. That is a 1.6x expansion from where we are now — at today's incomes, no growth assumed.

And here is where it gets specific. T2 and T3 together account for 79% of all viable new store additions. T3 alone is 55% of the entire pipeline. The growth is not in Metro. Metro is done. The next 20,000 stores belong to T2 and T3.

## [SLIDE: 68% Of T3's Potential Is Still Untouched]
*~45 seconds*

Look at this chart. It shows what percentage of each tier's total store potential has already been built.

Metro — 80% built. T1 — 83% built. Both essentially full.

T2 — only 59% built. T3 — only 32% built.

68% of T3's entire capacity for organized stores has not been built yet. The industry is barely through T3's potential. This is not the end of a growth story. It is the middle of it.

---

## [SLIDE: At Full Potential, 64% Of Pincodes Still Have 5 Stores or Fewer]
*~40 seconds*

The obvious question is — does the market ever get saturated?

Right now, 80% of India's postal codes have zero organized retail. At full potential — when every viable location has been served — 78% of postal codes would have at least one store, up from 20% today.

But here is what makes this market structurally different. Even at maximum build-out, 64% of postal codes still have 5 stores or fewer. The market never becomes crowded. There is enough spending spread across enough geography that it absorbs the growth without cannibalizing existing stores.

22% of postal codes are permanently unviable — the spending there is simply too thin at current income levels. That is the real ceiling. Everything else is open.

## [SLIDE: 11 Brands Are Riding The T2/T3 Wave!]
*~40 seconds*

Of over 100 organized apparel brands in India, only 11 are structurally positioned for the T2/T3 shift. 6 of those 11 are in value fashion.

Now look at the table. Zudio earns $1.34 million per store. V-Mart earns $0.56 million. Vishal Mega Mart earns $0.72 million. Zudio earns more than double its nearest value fashion peer per store.

But here is the honest read of this table. Zudio has 60% of its stores in Metro and T1 today. Its peers — V-Mart, Vishal, V2, Style Bazaar — are 69 to 78% T2 and T3. Zudio is the most productive player in the segment. But it is not yet the most geographically positioned for the opportunity it says it's chasing.

## [SLIDE: Zudio Can Add 902 More Stores At Current Economics]
*~40 seconds*

Our model says Zudio can grow from 958 stores today to 1,860. That is 902 additional stores — a 2x expansion — at exactly today's store economics. No revenue growth assumed. No income tailwind.

Of those 902 additional stores, 56% should go to T3. That is where the wallet is and where the competition is thinnest.

The right chart breaks this down by expansion type. 56% of Zudio's additions come from going deeper into cities it's already in — new postal codes within existing cities. 38% come from entering new cities entirely. The T3 story is primarily a new cities story.

## [SLIDE: $2.27 Billion Of Zudio's Revenue Potential Has Not Been Built]
*~50 seconds*

And here is what that means in revenue.

Zudio generated $1.29 billion in FY26. At full store potential, the ceiling is $3.55 billion. The gap — $2.27 billion — is the revenue that has not been built yet.

T3 alone tells the clearest story. Zudio is only 19% penetrated in T3. 506 stores of runway remain against 157 today. $814 million of revenue is still to be captured from T3 alone.

On pace — to reach the full 1,860 stores in 10 years requires 90 new stores per year. Zudio is currently adding well above that. The pace is not the problem.

And one final data point that the market may not be pricing in. Trent's own management guided for 5,000 Zudio stores at 200 per year at their most recent AGM. Our 1,860 store estimate is conservative by that measure.
